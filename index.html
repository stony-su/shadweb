WGS (STC 0010): Near Misses in Science and Art

By Jesse Yeow

A former UBC SHAD from the year 1989, Waterloo CS professor Craig S. Kaplan came to SHAD today and explained Near Misses, which refers to the construction for which "human error" or precision is comparable to the "mathematical error" or accuracy.

In three different applications, professor Kaplan shows Near Misses in the construction of the pentagon, music interlude, and tilings by regular polygons.

Pentagon Construction: Through the construction of the Pentagon, which is a polygon with 5 equal sides, the angle derived from him was roughly 107.59°, an angle not discovered by Euclid in which he found that the interior angle of a pentagon is 108°, showing a near miss.

Musical Interlude: The musical Interlude in which the axiom, which is when the frequency ratio of 2:1 sounds "the same" which then can be arranged into a circle accordingly with the logarithm of frequency.

Tilings by Regular Polygons: The theory of mathematics in which shapes are placed together with no spaces or gaps between them. For example, Johnson solids in which there are 92 in existence (excluding Platonic and Archimedean solids), was proven by Zallager in 1969.

He explains that because there is 360° of angle in one point, it should be completely flat and yet it is convex, meaning that one of the polygons that make a Johnson solid is not a regular polygon.

With all of these ideas in mind, Kaplan's demonstrations show that Near Misses aid in discoveries in math, art, and beyond.

WGS (EV2-2069): Sketching

By Michael Yang

Matt, the guest speaker and life partner to Susan, came and helped us realize the importance of sketching. They say a picture is worth a million words and it's the best way to help you communicate.

"The person who draws the best picture of the problem gets the money to solve it," says Dan Roam, the author of The Back of the Napkin.

How to Draw the Best Picture: They use the minimum number of features to convey a concept. Matt says that sketching isn't about what details to add, but what details you leave out.

Matt used a figure from Understanding Comics: the more detail you add, the less people can relate to the character; the less detail you add, the more people can relate to the character. Matt says this is why good authors draw superheroes with little details but villains with a myriad of details.

Going back to how sketching can help communicate, Matt told us how one of his seminar attendees used the sketch method to convey his concept. These sketches don't have to be good and it is possible to label these sketches. Just drawing a sketch while somebody explains adds a huge level of participation and allows people to see how their idea is communicated.

To end, Matt urged us to sketch and emphasized how "you can use anything to sketch".
