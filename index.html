Quantum Workshop: Interferometers and Quantum Computing

By Jesse Yeow

Today‚Äôs the last day for the workshop, and today, the quantum mechanics workshop split it into five main parts: wave-particle duality, photon experiments and detection, proving photons, quantum fireworks through experiment, quantum computing, and applications. Shads at the workshop learned that quantum objects like photons and electrons exhibit both wave and particle behavior, depending on the experiment. Interferometers, such as the Mach-Zehnder, demonstrated wave-like interference, while the photoelectric effect and coincidence experiments confirmed the particle nature of light. Shads also learned how beam splitters and phase shifts reveal these dual behaviors. A highlight was the quantum fireworks experiment, which used interference to determine whether a firework was a dud without setting it off directly. Finally and foremost, Shads learned how quantum computing uses qubits and principles like superposition and entanglement to solve complex problems more efficiently than classical computers. Technologies like optical coherence tomography and quantum simulators show how theory translates to real-world applications. This workshop gave Shads hands-on insight into the powerful world at the quantum level.

Transportation and Construction Part 2 Electric Boogaloo

By Jesse Yeow

Waterloo Civil Engineer alumni, our dear Jon Martin, continues his presentation in civil engineering, and today, he covered key innovations in Civil Engineering for the better of society, and the math concepts in their construction.

These innovations that he explained are: basket weaved roads, which is a type of interchange where one highway interchanges itself, and double and turbo roundabouts, which help decrease the speeds of vehicles in which is minimizing the effects of accidents. In addition, Jon explains the math concept of centrifugal force where the friction force arises in a rotating motion. Building off centrifugal forces, he explains superelevation, a crucial design technique that counteracts the centrifugal forces when skidding and turning when travelling on a curve.
